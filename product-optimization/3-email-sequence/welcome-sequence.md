# 3-Email Welcome Sequence

**Goal:** Convert PDF downloaders into $280 buyers of the AI Automation Assistant.
**Tool:** ConvertKit, Beehiiv, or similar — any provider with simple delays.
**Target conversion:** 2–4% of subscribers buy within 7 days.

## Sequence overview

| Email | Day | Subject line | Job |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 0 (instant) | Your guide is here (and one thing to do first) | Deliver the magnet, set expectations, build trust |
| 2 | Day 2 | The mistake I made with my first automation | Story → lesson → soft pitch |
| 3 | Day 4 | Want the other 7 automations? | Direct offer with deadline |

After this sequence, subscribers move into the regular weekly newsletter list.

---

## Email 1 — Day 0 (instant on signup)

**Subject:** Your guide is here (and one thing to do first)
**Preview text:** The PDF is attached. Read this before you open it.

```
Hey,

github.com/[your-handle]/claude-automations-starter

Before you dive in, one thing:

Don't try to set up all five automations this weekend. You'll burn out

   That list shapes what I build next.

2. Wednesday I'll send you the story of the first automation I built —
   and the mistake that made me throw it out and start over. It's the
   single most useful thing I can tell you before you write your own.

[Your name]
```

**Why this works:**
- Confirms delivery (kills the #1 churn cause: "did the PDF actually arrive?")
- Reduces overwhelm (gives permission to do less)
- Asks for a reply (replies = email deliverability gold)
- Foreshadows email 2 (open-loop)
- Ends with a low-friction next step (3-min video, not "buy something")

## Email 2 — Day 2

**Subject:** The mistake I made with my first automation
**Preview text:** I shipped it, used it for a week, then deleted the whole thing.

```
A confession.

The first version of my inbox triage script was a disaster.

Not because the code was bad. The code worked. The problem was I built
it to do too much.

Original spec:
- Read every email
- Categorize into 12 folders
- Auto-reply to anything routine
- Auto-archive newsletters
- Forward stuff to my assistant
- Surface "things that need my attention" in a daily digest

I shipped it. Used it for a week. Then deleted the whole thing and
started over.

Here's what went wrong: I couldn't trust it.

Twelve folders meant I had to mentally check twelve folders. Auto-reply
meant I lived in fear of a hallucinated response going to a client.
Auto-archive meant I missed two important emails the first week.

The version I run now does ONE thing: it scores incoming email and
drafts replies for the high-revenue ones. I review the drafts. I hit
send. The automation is just doing the thinking, not the deciding.

That's the lesson, and it applies to every automation in the guide:

  Automate the thinking, not the deciding.

The thinking is what's slow and exhausting. The deciding is what's
risky. Keep the deciding.

This is why the AI Automation Assistant — the full 12-workflow system
I sell — has every workflow set up the same way. Drafts, not sends.
Suggestions, not actions. You stay in the loop.

If you've already played with the free five and you're thinking about
building more, I built the next seven for you. They're in the full
system:

  - Calendar audit (find the meetings that shouldn't exist)
  - Daily standup-with-yourself
  - Outreach drafter (5-prompt sequence per prospect)
  - Content repurposer (1 input → 4 platforms)
  - Customer support triage
  - Subscription audit
  - End-of-month financial summary

Plus the prompt library I use for all of them, the setup walkthrough,
and the buyer-only Discord where I drop new templates every couple of
weeks.

If you want it, it's $280 one-time and lifetime updates:
[gumroad link]

If you don't, that's fine — keep using the five from the guide. They
do most of the work.

Friday I'll send the last email in this sequence — what changes when
you switch your "automation mindset" from sending to drafting. Then
I'll be in your inbox roughly once a week.

Talk Friday,
[Your name]
```

**Why this works:**
- Story-driven (50%+ open rates are story-led, not feature-led)
- The "Automate the thinking, not the deciding" line is memorable and
  sharable — it's the one thing they'll quote back to you
- Pitches the product as the natural extension of the lesson, not a
  hard sell
- Ends with another open loop for email 3

## Implementation notes

**Discount code mechanics:**
- Create `WELCOME50` in Gumroad with a 5-day expiration set per subscriber
- ConvertKit/Beehiiv can pass dynamic deadlines; if not, run cohort sales
  monthly instead

**Tagging:**
- Tag everyone who downloads the PDF: `magnet:claude-automations`
- Tag clickers: `clicked:gumroad-ai-assistant`
- Tag buyers: `customer:ai-assistant`
- Suppress emails 2 and 3 for anyone who buys in email 1

**Metrics to watch (be honest with yourself):**
| Metric | Healthy | Warning |
|---|---|---|
| Email 1 open rate | >55% | <40% (deliverability problem) |
| Email 1 reply rate | >5% | <2% (CTA isn't compelling) |
| Email 2 click rate (to Gumroad) | >8% | <3% (story isn't landing) |
| Email 3 conversion | 1.5–4% | <1% (offer or audience mismatch) |
| Total sequence revenue per subscriber | >$3 | <$1 (rebuild) |

**Iteration cadence:**
- First 100 subscribers: don't change anything. You don't have signal yet.
- 100–300: tweak subject lines only.
- 300+: A/B test email 2's story. That's where most of the lift is.

## What comes after the sequence

Move subscribers to the **weekly newsletter list**. One email a week, every
Wednesday. Format:

- 1 thing I built or broke
- 1 thing I read
- 1 prompt or workflow worth stealing

That's the whole format. Keep it ruthlessly short. The newsletter is the
long-term retention asset — the welcome sequence is just the first sale.
